# Vinaigrette

To mix a new vinaigrette for purpose, build backward from the dish rather than
forward from a known recipe.

**Function describes what the vinaigrette is doing on the dish.**

- Coating leaves a thin film on the surface and is tasted immediately; the
    dressing has nowhere to hide.
- Absorbing means starch soaks the dressing in. Grains and pasta dilute both
    acid and fat as they absorb, so the dressing needs to be built with that
    loss in mind.
- Sauce or drizzle means the vinaigrette is spooned over protein or vegetables
    and must hold together on the plate; it needs a stable emulsion and enough
    body to cling.
- Rich ingredients require more acid. Use a lower oil:acid ratio (i.e. less oil,
    more acid) when a dish contains rich elements such as cheese, avocado, cured
    meat, fatty fish.

**Weight describes how substantially the base ingredients buffer acid and fat.**

- Delicate ingredients such as tender greens, fish, and light vegetables offer
    little buffering, so a sharp dressing overwhelms them.
- Medium-weight ingredients like cooked pasta, grains, roasted vegetables, and
    potatoes absorb some of the dressing and mute it, but only partially. Their
    starch or fiber dilutes the acid without fully neutralizing it.
- Hearty ingredients like beans, red meat, and charcuterie absorb and mute
    acidity, demanding a more assertive dressing to register at all.

## Oil-to-Acid Ratio

An undiluted vinaigrette should taste assertively tart, as starch, fat, and
protein in the dish will mellow it significantly.

| Function | Weight   | Oil:Acid Ratio |
| :------- | :------- | -------------: |
| Absorb   | Delicate |      3:1 - 7:2 |
| Absorb   | Medium   |      5:2 - 3:1 |
| Absorb   | Hearty   |      2:1 - 5:2 |
| Coat     | Delicate |      7:2 - 4:1 |
| Coat     | Medium   |            3:1 |
| Coat     | Hearty   |      5:2 - 3:1 |
| Sauce    | Delicate |            3:1 |
| Sauce    | Medium   |            5:2 |
| Sauce    | Hearty   |            2:1 |

## When to Dress

Dress warm when you want the dish to absorb the vinaigrette, and dress when
serving otherwise.

**Dress pasta in two stages.** While there's typically no fuss to answer when to
dress, pasta and grains are the exception.

1. Add two-thirds of the dressing to hot, just-drained pasta.
2. Reserve one-third to add at serving.

## Flavor direction

Use the below table as a basic guide when you're unsure. Cut non-neutral oils
with neutral oils for more mild flavors. Aromatics and other flavorings are left
to you.

| Direction          | Oils                             | Acids                                 | Emulsifiers                   |
| :----------------- | :------------------------------- | :------------------------------------ | :---------------------------- |
| Euro-Continental   | Olive oil                        | White wine vinegar; sherry vinegar    | Dijon; shallot                |
| Euro-Mediterranean | Olive oil                        | Red wine vinegar; white wine vinegar  | Dijon & garlic; shallot       |
| Middle Eastern     | Olive oil                        | Lemon juice & pomegranate molasses    | Pomegranate molasses & garlic |
| Scandinavian       | Neutral oil                      | White wine; apple cider vinegar       | Whole grain mustard           |
| Indian             | Neutral oil                      | Lemon juice; tamarind                 | Garlic; ginger paste          |
| Japanese           | Neutral oil & toasted sesame oil | Rice vinegar                          | White miso & garlic           |
| Chinese / Sichuan  | Sichuan chili oil & sesame oil   | Black vinegar; rice vinegar           | Garlic paste                  |
| Southeast Asian    | Neutral oil                      | Lime juice & fish sauce               | Garlic                        |
| Latin American     | Neutral oil                      | Lime juice                            | Dijon & garlic                |
| American           | Olive oil                        | Apple cider vinegar; balsamic vinegar | Dijon & shallot               |

## Calibration

When you're really unsure:

1. **Season the acid first.** Salt dissolves in acid, not oil.
2. **Mix in the emulsifier.**
3. **Taste the acid mixture alone.** It should sting.
4. **Stream in oil** while whisking. Taste undiluted and adjust.
    - too sharp → more oil
    - too flat → more salt or acid
    - missing depth → more emulsifier or a pinch of sweetener
