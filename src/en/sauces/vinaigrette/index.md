# Vinaigrette

To customize a vinaigrette, build backward from the dish, not forward from a
default recipe.

## Ratio and timing

An undiluted vinaigrette should taste assertively tart, as starch, fat, and
protein in the dish will mellow it significantly.

| Weight   | Function | Oil:Acid Ratio | Timing                          |
| -------- | -------- | -------------- | ------------------------------- |
| Delicate | Coat     | 7:2 - 4:1      | Dress at service                |
| Delicate | Absorb   | 3:1 - 7:2      | Dress warm                      |
| Delicate | Sauce    | 3:1            | Emulsify well                   |
| Medium   | Coat     | 3:1            | Dress at service                |
| Medium   | Absorb   | 5:2 - 3:1      | Dress warm; two-stage for pasta |
| Medium   | Sauce    | 5:2            | Emulsify well                   |
| Hearty   | Coat     | 5:2 - 3:1      | Dress at service                |
| Hearty   | Absorb   | 2:1 - 5:2      | Dress warm                      |
| Hearty   | Sauce    | 2:1            | Emulsify firmly                 |

## Weight and function

**Weight describes how substantially the base ingredients buffer acid and fat.**

- Delicate ingredients such as tender greens, fish, and light vegetables offer
    little buffering, so a sharp dressing overwhelms them.
- Medium-weight ingredients like cooked pasta, grains, roasted vegetables, and
    potatoes absorb some of the dressing and mute it, but only partially. Their
    starch or fiber dilutes the acid without fully neutralizing it.
- Hearty ingredients like beans, red meat, and charcuterie absorb and mute
    acidity, demanding a more assertive dressing to register at all.

**Function describes what the vinaigrette is doing on the dish.**

- Coating leaves a thin film on the surface and is tasted immediately; the
    dressing has nowhere to hide.
- Absorbing means starch soaks the dressing in. Grains and pasta dilute both
    acid and fat as they absorb, so the dressing needs to be built with that
    loss in mind.
- Sauce or drizzle means the vinaigrette is spooned over protein or vegetables
    and must hold together on the plate; it needs a stable emulsion and enough
    body to cling.

!!! tip "Rich ingredients require more acid"

    Use a lower oil:acid ratio (i.e. less oil, more acid) when a dish contains rich
    elements such as cheese, avocado, cured meat, fatty fish.

!!! tip "Dress pasta in two stages"

    Warm starch absorbs the dressing rather than repelling it.

    1. Add two-thirds of the dressing to hot, just-drained pasta.
    2. Reserve one-third to add at serving.

## Flavor direction

| Direction                  | Acid                               | Oil                            | Aromatics                                    |
| -------------------------- | ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------- |
| Mediterranean / Italian    | Red or white wine vinegar          | Olive oil                      | Garlic, shallot, oregano                     |
| Middle Eastern / Levantine | Lemon juice + pomegranate molasses | Olive oil                      | Garlic, sumac, dried mint                    |
| Central European / French  | White wine or sherry vinegar       | Walnut oil or Olive oil        | Shallot, tarragon, thyme                     |
| Scandinavian / Nordic      | White wine or apple cider vinegar  | Rapeseed oil or neutral oil    | Whole grain mustard, dill, honey             |
| Indian                     | Lemon juice or tamarind            | Mustard oil or neutral oil     | Cumin, ginger, green chili, fresh coriander  |
| Japanese                   | Rice vinegar                       | Neutral oil + toasted sesame   | Mirin, soy, ginger; white miso as emulsifier |
| Chinese / Sichuan          | Black vinegar (or rice vinegar)    | Sichuan chili oil + sesame oil | Garlic, scallion, Sichuan peppercorn, soy    |
| Southeast Asian            | Lime juice + fish sauce            | Neutral oil                    | Lemongrass, chili, cilantro, garlic          |
| Latin American             | Lime juice                         | Avocado oil or neutral oil     | Garlic, cilantro, cumin                      |
| American / summer          | Apple cider or balsamic            | Olive oil                      | Shallot, chive, honey                        |

Cut EVOO with neutral oil to dial back intensity -- a 50/50 blend approximates a
mild EVOO.

!!! note "Pomegranate molasses"

    Functions as both acid and emulsifier; i.e., thick enough to bind the dressing
    without mustard. Steep sumac in a small amount of hot water for 10 minutes
    before adding to open up its flavor.

!!! note "Scandinavian whole grain mustard"

    Serves as both emulsifier and primary aromatic; Dijon is a poor substitute here
    as it lacks the textural and flavor character.

## Calibration

1. **Season the acid first.** Salt dissolves in acid, not oil.
2. **Add the emulsifier.** Dijon for most directions; white miso for Japanese;
    fish sauce provides loose binding for Southeast Asian; Indian and Sichuan
    traditionally use none.
3. **Taste the acid mixture alone.** It should sting.
4. **Stream in oil** while whisking. Taste undiluted and adjust: too sharp →
    more oil; too flat → more salt or acid; missing depth → more emulsifier or
    a pinch of sweetener.
