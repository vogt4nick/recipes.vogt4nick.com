=== "Ingredients"

    - 500 g Fruit, crushed, chopped, etc.
    - 250 g Sugar
    - Aromatics (optional)
    - Vinegar, to taste

    ??? tip "Crush and chop fruit to extract more juice."

        - Crush soft berries lightly to open the cells.
        - Roughly chop stone fruit (pitted) and strawberries.
        - Grate or finely chop hard fruit like apples or pears.

    ??? tip "Choose no more than 2 aromatics."

        - Fresh ginger, peeled, sliced thin
        - Citrus zest
        - Whole spices like peppercorns, cardamom pods, cinnamon bark, star anise,
          cloves
        - Fresh woody herbs such as rosemary, thyme
        - Fresh soft herbs such as basil, mint (remove at straining)
        - Dried flowers such as rose petals, lavender buds

    ??? warning "Use commercially bottled vinegar labelled at least 5% acidity."

        "5% acidity" is a concentration measure (% acetic acid by weight/volume), not a
        pH reading. 5% acetic acid sits at roughly pH 2.4-2.6. On some labels this
        appears as "50 grain" instead.[^psu]

        Check the label for a statement such as "diluted with water to X% acidity". Do
        not use homemade vinegar. Its acidity is uncontrolled and cannot be relied on
        for safe preservation.[^iastate]

=== "Directions"

    1. **Combine.** Fold fruit and sugar together in a clean non-reactive bowl or
        jar so the sugar coats the fruit. Add any aromatics. Cover tightly.
    2. **Wait.** Leave at room temperature for 48 hours, stirring once daily. The
        sugar draws out the fruit juices and dissolves into a syrup.
    3. **Strain.** Pour through a fine-mesh sieve into a measuring jug, pressing
        firmly on the solids. Note the volume. Discard the spent fruit or reserve
        for dessert use.
    4. **Add vinegar.** Starting at 3/4 the measured syrup volume, add vinegar
        slowly and taste as you go. Adjust up to equal volume. The finished shrub
        should be sweet-tart -- sharp enough to make you blink, sweet enough to
        keep you sipping.[^food52] Tart fruits (raspberry, rhubarb, citrus) want
        less vinegar; mild fruits (pear, melon) take more.
    5. **Bottle.** Pour into a sterilised glass jar or bottle. Seal and refrigerate.
        The flavour mellows and rounds out over the first 3-4 days; taste before
        serving.[^greatbritishchefs]
    6. **Serve.** Mix roughly 1 part shrub to 6-8 parts sparkling water.

    ??? tip "Macerate in the refrigerator if your kitchen is warm."

        Above 25°C / 77°F, refrigerate instead. Expect 3-5 days rather than 48 hours --
        slower, but the results are clean.[^askextension]

=== "Pairing Ideas"

    | Fruit      | Sugar    | Vinegar                     | Aromatics             |
    | ---------- | -------- | --------------------------- | --------------------- |
    | Raspberry  | White    | Apple cider                 | Black peppercorn      |
    | Strawberry | White    | Champagne                   | Basil                 |
    | Peach      | Brown    | Apple cider                 | Fresh ginger, thyme   |
    | Blueberry  | White    | Apple cider + dash balsamic | Lavender              |
    | Plum       | Brown    | Red wine                    | Star anise            |
    | Apple      | Demerara | Apple cider                 | Cinnamon, cardamom    |
    | Blackberry | White    | Red wine                    | Rosemary              |
    | Pineapple  | White    | White wine                  | Fresh ginger, chilli  |
    | Cucumber   | White    | Champagne                   | Dill, pink peppercorn |

    ??? question "How do I choose a sugar?"

        - White caster sugar lets the fruit flavour lead.
        - Brown sugar or Demerara adds caramel and molasses depth, and pairs well with
          apple, pear, or stone fruit.
        - Honey is floral and complex; pairs well with stone fruit, berries, and herbs.
        - Coconut sugar and maple syrup substitute freely -- keep the same weight
          ratio.[^greatbritishchefs]

    ??? question "How do I choose a vinegar?"

        - **Apple cider vinegar** is the most versatile; mild, fruity, slightly funky.
        - **White wine or Champagne vinegar** are clean and light; good with delicate
          fruit such as white peach, pear, or melon.
        - **Red wine vinegar** is robust; suits berries and stone fruit.
        - **Rice vinegar** has gentle acidity; good with Asian-inspired shrubs featuring
          ginger, lemongrass, or pineapple.
        - **Balsamic** -- use a splash only alongside another vinegar; extraordinary
          with dark berries.[^food52]

        Plain white distilled vinegar is too aggressive and one-dimensional -- avoid
        it.[^food52]

!!! info "Keeps up to 6 months refrigerated.[^askextension]"

??? question "How do I choose a fruit?"

    Best results come from ripe -- even over-ripe fruit (as long as it shows no mold
    or rot).

    - Stone fruit and berries are the classic choice: strawberries, raspberries,
        blackberries, blueberries, peaches, plums, nectarines, apricots.
    - Harder fruits like apple, pear, melon, pomegranate, fig, and cranberry work
        well but may need extra maceration time or a quick pulse in a food processor
        to break down the cell walls.[^greatbritishchefs]
    - Vegetables also work: cucumber, beetroot, tomato, carrot; although these lean
        savoury and pair best with wine or cider vinegar.
