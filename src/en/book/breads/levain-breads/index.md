# Levain Breads

Levain breads are naturally leavened loaves that use a mature starter culture instead of commercial yeast. They depend on a living colony of wild yeasts and lactic acid bacteria that convert sugars into carbon dioxide, alcohol, and organic acids. These compounds create distinctive aroma, flavor, and structure.

This intro summarizes terminology, core concepts, and best practices for using a levain in bread formulas.

## Glossary


### Hydration Percentage

The **hydration percentage** is a calculation to clarify amounts recipes. The weight of water divided by the weight of flour, multiplied by 100. Hydration most noticeably influences fermentation speed and density.[^leo2][^prior]

- 55% hydration doughs like [pain de mie](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/recipes/classic-pain-de-mie-recipe) are often tight, uniform crumb; ideal for sandwich loaves.
- 65% hydration doughs like _The Perfect Loaf_'s [Rustic Country Loaf](https://www.theperfectloaf.com/rustic-sourdough-bread-recipe/) are standard artisan doughs.
- 80% hydration doughs like [ciabatta](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/recipes/ciabatta-recipe) are often light, open crumb.


### Levain Preferment

A **levain preferment** is a mixture built from the mother culture for a single-use recipe. It often contains flour, water, and a small inoculation of mature mother culture. Levain is mixed at specific hydration and allowed to ferment before being incorporated into the final mix.[^leo]


### Prefermented Flour Fraction (PFF)

A dough's **prefermented flour fraction (PFF)** is the proportion of total flour in the final dough that is fermented in the levain. This metric is widely used to standardize formulas.

### Mother Culture

A **mother culture** (a.k.a. **starter**) is the ongoing yeast colony you maintain. It provides the microbial population that drives fermentation in the levain and dough.[^anastopoulo]

??? tip "A starter is not a levain!"
    Web personalities may mistakenly say "starter" when they mean levain preferment. Although it's often clear from context, DO NOT CONFUSE mother culture and levain preferments--they are very different mixtures!"


### Inoculation Rate

The **inoculation rate** (a.k.a. **inoculation percentage**) is the ratio of mother culture to new flour in a levain preferment. For example, 10 g mother culture and 90 g fresh flour sums to 100 g, making the inoculation rate 10%. While the mother culture contains flour and water, the inoculation rate uses the total, combined weight of flour and water in the mother culture.

A higher inoculation rate causes faster fermentation, milder acidity; a lower inoculation rate causes slower fermentation, deeper acidity.[^clark]

- A 2-5% inoculation rate encourages mild acid development over longer times.
- A 5-20% inoculation rate is common for many recipes.[^janjigian]
- A 20-30% inoculation rate speeds fermentation and somewhat increases acidity.


## Reading a Baker’s Table

Levain formulas are expressed in **baker’s percentages**, where total flour = 100 %. All other ingredients are listed as percentages of total flour weight. This allows scaling of any recipe while keeping ingredient ratios fixed. Danielle Ellis [explains](https://severnbites.com/2018/05/26/bake-better-take-control-use-bakers-percentages/) baker's percentages in detail.


## Key Breadmaking Stages

Understanding each stage in levain bread production helps control flavor, texture, and rise. Below is a typical sequence with emphasis on function:

1. **Autolyse** promotes gluten development, enzymatic activity, and dough extensibility.
2. **Mixing and/or Kneading** distributes microbes evenly, develops gluten network for structure.
3. **Bulk Fermentation** (a.k.a. first rise) builds gas, flavor, and dough strength. Stretch-and-folds can be used to strengthen gluten without intensive kneading.
4. **Shaping** creates surface tension, partially degasses, prepares for final rise.
5. **Proofing or Final Fermentation** (a.k.a. second rise) allows yeast to expand dough fully and lactic-acid bacteria to continue developing flavor; may occur at room temperature or in cold retardation.
6. **Cold Retardation** slows yeast activity while allowing LAB to produce acids, enhancing tangy flavor without overproofing.
7. **Scoring** controls expansion, prevents tearing, and influences final loaf shape.
8. **Baking** produces the characteristic oven spring, gelatinization of starches, Maillard reaction for crust and color.
9. **Cooling** sets the crumb, redistributes moisture, and stabilizes flavor.
